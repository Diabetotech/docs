## **<u>2. Comparison of different insulin pumps</u>**

Welcome to this lesson, where we will explore the various insulin pumps available in the market.

In this video, we will cover tubed pumps and patch pumps, and essential considerations to keep in mind while choosing the right insulin pump for your needs.

Insulin pumps, small yet power-packed devices, carry within them a reservoir of insulin, a pumping mechanism, a tiny computer and a battery. They work with rapid-acting insulin, delivering it at a steady, low rate as basal insulin, or in larger doses as a bolus during meals or when a correction dose is needed. W<span class="mark">hile the insulin used is generally Novorapid, depending on the manufacturer’s labelling</span> and/or a healthcare professional’s discretion, Humalog, NovoRapid, NovoLog, Apidra, Fiasp, or Lyumjev may also be used.

Let's delve into the intriguing differences among various insulin pumps.

Essentially, we have two categories - tubed pumps and patch pumps.

- Think of tubed pumps as little command devices. They come with inbuilt software, buttons, and a screen for pump control. These devices channel insulin through a length of tubing to an infusion set attached to your body, infusing it into your subcutaneous fat tissue via a cannula. Every 2 to 7 days, you need to replace the infusion set and the cannula to avoid infection and maintain control of glucose levels. The pump has to be worn 24/7, though it can be disconnected for up to 2 hours at a time for bathing, intimacy, or some types of exercise. It can be clipped to your clothing or placed beside you while sleeping.

- On the other hand, patch pumps are streamlined units consisting of only the insulin, the pump mech<span class="mark">anism, and a battery. They are controlled by an external remote control or potentially a mobile phone. Affixed directly to your body, a patch pump needs to be replaced every three days just like most of the infusion sets of a tubed pump. They may offer more freedom in daily activities as there's no need to worry about the risk of tubing getting caught. However, you do need to carry a remote control or mobile phone all the time. And once you apply a patch pump, you have it on in that body site for 3 days, which may be more visible than a tubed pump, depending on what outfits</span> you wear.

### ***2.1 Tubed pumps***

Popular tubed p<span class="mark">umps include the MiniMed 780G, Tandem t:slim X2, Tandem Mobi, Kaleido, mylife Ypsopump, iLet Device, Dana pump and the twiist Pump.</span>

- <span class="mark">The MiniMed pump is the largest tubed pump, although size is usually not that big a deal if someone needs the features of a specific pump. The mylife YpsoPump and Dana pump are smaller tubed options. The Tandem Mobi, Kaleido, and twiist pumps are tubed systems that can also be worn on the body. The Tandem Mobi and Kaleido also offer shorter tubes, allowing the pump to be placed directly next to the infusion set.</span>

- <span class="mark">The majority of these tubed pumps have replaceable batteries that usually last for 1 to 4 weeks. On the other hand, the Tandem, Kaleido, and iLet pumps have a rechargeable battery, offering a more environmentally friendly option, but requiring regular charging.</span>

- <span class="mark">Insulin reservoirs are typically filled by hand from a vial of insulin, but the mylife YpsoPump and iLet Device can also be used with prefilled insulin PumpCarts. These PumpCarts hold only 160 units of insulin, so th</span>ey need replacing more frequently than reservoirs like the MiniMed pump, which can store 300 units.

- Tubed pumps usually have a screen and buttons to adjust insulin delivery. <span class="mark">The Tandem t:slim X2 pump, mylife YpsoPump, and iLet device feature a touchscreen, while most other pumps rely on physical buttons for control. The Tandem Mobi, twiist pump, and Kaleido are app-controlled via smartphone, though both the Tandem Mobi and twiist pump also include a single button on the pump itself.</span>

- <span class="mark">These pumps can all be integrated with a sensor or algorithm via Bluetooth, which is why they're often used in automated insulin delivery systems today. ### ***2.2 Patch pumps***

Patch <span class="mark">pumps include the Omnipod DASH and Omnipod 5, TouchCare Nano pump, and the Equil pump.</span>

<span class="mark">Patch pumps are operated with a remote control or with an app on your phone.</span>

- <span class="mark">Always check whether your mobile phone is compatible and if the app is available in your country. For Omnipod 5 for example the app is only available in the US.</span>

<span class="mark">Most patch pumps are modular, with a reusable pump base that typically lasts up to 4 years, while the insulin reservoir needs to be replaced every 3 days. The Omnipod pumps are an exception, as they are fully discarded every 3 days.</span>

***2.3 Choosing an insulin pump***

When choosing a pump, it's crucial to consider a few things.

- <span class="mark">Check which insulin pumps are covered by your health insurance. Not all automated insulin delivery systems are reimbursed in all countries or even all regions or hospitals within the same country. Also, consult your diabetes team on what technology they can offer that best suits your needs. Important considerations include the pump's ability to function as an automated insulin delivery system, your preference for a patch or tubed pump, size and weight of the pump, and needing to carry a remote control or mobile phone to control the pump.</span>

- <span class="mark">It's important to protect your pump and remote control appropriately. A silicone cover and screen protector are usually included and are recommended for use. A crack in your pump means it's no longer waterproof and needs to be replaced. Insulin pump systems typically have a warranty period of 4 years.</span>

- <span class="mark">Tubed pumps offer a variety of wearing options, and there's an array of helpful accessories such as customized pouches, belts, and specially-designed clothing and underwear. Many companies typically provide a belt clip or a holder at no extra cost. Remember to keep your pump within reach as you will need frequent access. Be sure to explore the range of unique and practical options available in various online specialty stores.</span>

- <span class="mark">When it comes to showering or bathing, most users choose to disconnect their tubed pump at the infusion set. For intimate activities, you have the option of either letting your pump hang if your catheter is long enough, or disconnecting it. Be mindful, however, that your pump should not be disconnected for more than two hours, as this could leave you without insulin, increasing the risk of ketoacidosis.</span>

- <span class="mark">Lastly, bear in mind that the main issue with insulin pumps is making sure the insulin is getting into the body safely. Most infusion sets need to be changed every 3 days to reduce the risk of an infection and keep glucose levels in control. Going without insulin because it’s not being absorbed from the infusion site can increase the risk of diabetic ketoacidosis, which is a serious health emergency.</span>

<span class="mark">Now you have a general feeling on how insulin pumps work, which insulin pumps are available and what the differences are. T</span>his may seem like a lot of information, but once you really get to grips with it, you will see that each insulin pump works largely the same and is reasonably intuitive. Most people on an insulin pump will be perfectly capable of operating their pump themselves, after all, they work with it 24/7. So don't be worried about starting insulin pump therapy. Think of it like using a mobile phone, once you get the hang of it, the functionality becomes second nature.
