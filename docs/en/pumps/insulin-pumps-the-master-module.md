## **<u>2. Comparison of different insulin pumps</u>**

Welcome to this lesson, where we will explore the various insulin pumps available in the market.

In this video, we will cover tubed pumps and patch pumps, and essential considerations to keep in mind while choosing the right insulin pump for your needs.

Insulin pumps, small yet power-packed devices, carry within them a reservoir of insulin, a pumping mechanism, a tiny computer and a battery. They work with rapid-acting insulin, delivering it at a steady, low rate as basal insulin, or in larger doses as a bolus during meals or when a correction dose is needed. W<span class="mark">hile the insulin used is generally Novorapid, depending on the manufacturer’s labelling</span> and/or a healthcare professional’s discretion, Humalog, NovoRapid, NovoLog, Apidra, Fiasp, or Lyumjev may also be used.

Let's delve into the intriguing differences among various insulin pumps.

Essentially, we have two categories - tubed pumps and patch pumps.

- Think of tubed pumps as little command devices. They come with inbuilt software, buttons, and a screen for pump control. These devices channel insulin through a length of tubing to an infusion set attached to your body, infusing it into your subcutaneous fat tissue via a cannula. Every 2 to 7 days, you need to replace the infusion set and the cannula to avoid infection and maintain control of glucose levels. The pump has to be worn 24/7, though it can be disconnected for up to 2 hours at a time for bathing, intimacy, or some types of exercise. It can be clipped to your clothing or placed beside you while sleeping.

- On the other hand, patch pumps are streamlined units consisting of only the insulin, the pump mech<span class="mark">anism, and a battery. They are controlled by an external remote control or potentially a mobile phone. Affixed directly to your body, a patch pump needs to be replaced every three days just like most of the infusion sets of a tubed pump. They may offer more freedom in daily activities as there's no need to worry about the risk of tubing getting caught. However, you do need to carry a remote control or mobile phone all the time. And once you apply a patch pump, you have it on in that body site for 3 days, which may be more visible than a tubed pump, depending on what outfits</span> you wear.

## **<u>3. Insulin settings</u>**

Welcome to this comprehensive video about insulin pump settings!

An insulin pump offers an intricate way to deliver insulin. It allows you to control both your basal and bolus insulin levels with only short acting insulin.

In this discussion, we'll explore the various settings and how to set basal and bolus insulin settings as you switch from insulin pens to a pump.

It's important to note that most guidelines about insulin pump settings are opinion-based, and that these videos are not intended as medical advice. Always consult with your healthcare provider before changing your treatment.

### ***3.1 Overview of insulin settings***

In an insulin pump, you can adjust basal insulin delivery for each half-hour or hour-long segment throughout your day.

- In most pumps, you can set up multiple different basic profiles to select from.

- There's also a feature to choose a temporary basal rate, which allows you to adjust your insulin release for a specific period of time.

- You can also stop or suspend your insulin pump at any time. However, be cautious about stopping the pump for longer than two hours, as it could lead to ketoacidosis.

As for bolus insulin, the pump gives two options:

- a manual bolus, which lets you determine the insulin amount you want to administer,

- and a bolus from the bolus calculator, which computes the necessary insulin based on your carbohydrate intake, blood sugar level, carbohydrate ratio, insulin sensitivity, target value, and active insulin.

Furthermore, you can choose among various types of boluses <span class="mark">like a simple bolus, an extended bolus, or a combined bolus,</span> that could be particularly useful for meals high in fat.

### ***3.2 Basal insulin rate***

Most individuals with type 1 diabetes using insulin pens follow a basal-bolus regimen. This consists of one or two injections of long-acting insulin and three or mor<span class="mark">e injections of rapid-acting insulin for mealtime carbohydrate intake.</span>

<span class="mark">In transitioning to an insulin pump, we generally calculate the total daily insulin dose, by making the sum of both long and short-acting insulin, and set the pump to deliver 80% of this total daily insulin dose. This reduced dose is because the insulin seems to work more efficiently via the pump, and we want to avoid hypoglycemia.</span>

*<span class="mark">As an example, let's consider Anne, who has type 1 diabetes. Every day, she injects 45 units of Lantus before bed and on average, she has a total of 30 units of Novorapid with her meals. That's 75 units in total. If she switches to an insulin pump, she'll need to reduce her total daily insulin dose to 60 units.</span>*

<span class="mark">This insulin amount is then divided into doses of basal and bolus insulin to be given by the insulin pump. For most people, the starting ratio of basal to bolus is about 50/50%, but it can vary depending on individual dietary habits. This means that the basal daily insulin dose will be half of the previously calculated total daily insulin dose.</span>

<span class="mark">Now that you know the total basal insulin dose, you can divide that by 24 and set that as a fixed hourly rate. In our previous example, we determined that Ann's total daily insulin requirement would be 60 units when switching to an insulin pump. From this, her total basal insulin need can be calculated to be 30 units per day. By dividing this amount by 24 hours, we arrive at a calculated basal insulin rate of 1.25 units per hour. Most healthcare providers are accustomed to further fine-tuning the basal rate to align with the circadian insulin needs of their patients.</span>

- <span class="mark">For manual insulin pumps, it is common practice to set a lower basal insulin rate at night, for example 20% lower, and a higher basal rate during the day to better match the body's varying insulin requirements.</span>

- <span class="mark">For people with known dawn phenomenon, a higher basal rate can be set between, say, 2AM and 7AM.</span>

<span class="mark">To evaluate basal insulin, you can monitor blood sugar levels. This can be done either with finger sticks or a glucose sensor, but using a glucose sensor makes the process easier.</span>

- <span class="mark">At night, this is relatively straightforward as no additional factors like eating or exercising affect your blood sugar.</span>

- <span class="mark">During the day, it's a little more complex. One way is to occasionally skip or delay a meal for a few hours and see if there's a persistent change in blood sugar levels. A rise or fall of more than 30 mg/dl (or 1.7 mmol/l), may indicate a need for basal insulin adjustment.</span>

Once the basal rates are set, the bolus calculator parameters can also be defined.

### ***3.3 Bolus calculator parameters***

Bolus calculator parameters include the carbohydrate ratio, insulin sensitivity factor (also called correction factor), bolus calculator target value, and duration of insulin action. We’ll discuss them one by one.

#### ***Insulin-to-carbohydrate ratio***

The Insulin-to-Carbohydrate ratio or carb ratio is the quantity of carbohydrates that c<span class="mark">an be covered by one unit of insulin. To determine the amount of insulin to bolus for a set amount of carbs, patients need to count their carbohydrate intake during meals and then divide it by the calculated carb ratio.</span>

<span class="mark">For example if you're going to eat 50 grams of carbs and your carb ratio is 5 grams per unit, you will need to bolus 10 units of insulin.</span>

<span class="mark">The ratio can be established in a few ways:</span>

- by employing the 450-rule,

- through a dietician who calculates the carbohydrate intake per meal and then divides it by the amount of insulin units injected,

- or by taking a certain percentage of the total daily insulin dose.

The 450-rule means that you can divide 450 by the total daily insulin dose to calculate the carb ratio.

*For example, if Ann’s total daily insulin dose was 60 units, and using the 450-rule, her carbohydrate ratio would be 7.5 grams per unit. Hence, for a meal of 15 grams, she would need 2 units of insulin.*

Alternatively, some healthcare providers use the 300 to 500-rule to calculate the carb ratio.

Another method involves a dietician calculating the actual carbohydrate intake, which is then divided by the units of insulin injected.

Lastly, one can take a certain percentage of the total daily dose for meal boluses. In the case of fixed portion meals, it is typically recommended to use 50% of the total daily insulin dose for all meals. Given that more insulin is usually required during breakfast, it can be divided as follows: 20% for breakfast, 15% for lunch, and 15% for dinner.

To evaluate the carbohydrate ratio, check the blood sugar level 3-5 hours after an insulin bolus. It should return to pre-meal levels. If it is consistently higher or lower, consider adjusting the carbohydrate ratio by 10%.

#### ***Insulin sensitivity***

The second parameter for the bolus calculator is insulin sensitivity. This reflects the amount the blood sugar level drops per unit of insulin, measured in mg/dl or mmol/l. The 1800-rule (or 100-rule in mmol/l) is commonly used, but a more aggressive setting could be the 1500-rule (or 90-rule), and a less aggressive setting might be the 2000-rule (or 120 to even 130-rule).

*Take Ann’s case for example: with a total daily insulin dose of 60 units, her insulin sensitivity can be calculated using the 1800-rule. 1800 divided by 60 equals 30. Therefore, setting the insulin sensitivity to 30 mg/dl per unit means that a unit of insulin will decrease the blood sugar level by 30 mg/dl.*

#### ***Glucose target*** 

The bolus calculator also requires a glucose target, which could be a fixed number or a target range.

Depending on the type of insulin pump, the bolus calculator may use the target value differently.

- Most pumps will correct to the middle of the target range.

- Some pumps, like the Omnipod DASH, also have a 'Correct Above' value, indicating when a correction will be applied.

- Some pumps also offer an option for reverse correction, subtracting insulin if the current blood sugar level is lower than the target.

It is generally recommended to set a fixed target value or a target rang<span class="mark">e of 90-100 mg/dl (5-5.5 mmol/l). However, this should be individualized.</span>

#### ***Duration of insulin action***

Lastly, you need to set the duration of insulin action. This parameter determines how long the insulin stays active, and thus how much of the previous insulin bolus is still in effect. To avoid insulin stacking, it's crucial for the pump to take this into account. Typically, this is set to 3 to 5 hours.

So now you understand how to set the basal insulin and the parameters for the bolus calculator.

### ***3.4 Do's & don’ts***

- On the first day of using an insulin pump, basal insulin from the insulin pens may still be present. It is recommended to inject only half the long-acting insulin the day before starting the pump. For users of Toujeo or Tresiba, it may be best to switch to Lantus a week in advance due to its shorter action profile. If unsure, it's safer to reduce the basal insulin rate when starting the insulin pump to prevent hypoglycemia.

- When starting on an insulin pump, it's usually necessary to set a limit for insulin delivery, i.e., the maximum basal and maximum bolus. Typically, it's recommended that the maximum basal insulin delivery should be double the highest set basal insulin delivery, and the maximum bolus should be the largest bolus expected for a large meal.

- All insulin pumps allow users to set various profiles for different situations. The number of basal profiles that can be set varies depending on the pump. Some pumps only store the basal insulin in a profile, while others, like the Tandem Pu<span class="mark">mps, al</span>so allow adjustment of the carbohydrate ratio and insulin sensitivity per profile.

- There are also several strategies for adjusting your insulin needs with temporary basal rates.

  - These should be evaluated individually. In some cases you could consider a temporary higher basal rate, for instance:

    - a 50% higher basal rate for 8 hours after eating a high-fat meal

    - an 80% higher basal rate for when you’re ill

    - a 50% higher basal rate 3-6 hours after receiving a corticosteroid injection

    - an 80% higher basal rate from 1-2 hours before a scheduled stressful event

    - a 40% higher basal rate during prolonged inactivity, like a long car ride

    - <span class="mark">a 50% higher basal rate in the week before your menstruation, some people also set a 30% lower basal rate during their menstruation</span>

  - In other cases you can consider a lower temporary basal rate, for instance:

    - a 50% lower basal rate from <span class="mark">1-2</span> hours before extensive activity like housework or yard work

    - a 30% lower basal rate for 6-10 hours after exercise to prevent late onset hypoglycemia

    - or a 40% lower basal rate after consuming alcohol for 2 hours per drink

Lastly, remember to constantly monitor your blood sugar level. The insulin pump's primary advantage is the precision and customization it offers. To make the most of it, you should adjust settings based on the observed patterns. You may also need to tweak these settings frequently, at least in the beginning, to achieve optimal glucose control.

Remember that every person is different, and while these guidelines may apply to many, there's no 'one-size-fits-all' solution in diabetes management. It requires personal fine-tuning and learning by trial and error under the supervision of your healthcare provider.

Keep in mind that while insulin pumps can make management easier and more precise, they also require a deeper understanding and commitment to managing your diabetes. In some ways, it can be more demanding because of the necessary knowledge and continual adjustments.

We hope this presentation has provided you with some valuable insights about transitioning from insulin pens to an insulin pump. We encourage you to reach out to your healthcare team for further support and guidance during this transition.

## **<u>4. Reservoir and infusion set replacement</u>**

Welcome to this lesson about reservoir and infusion set replacement in insulin pumps.

This tutorial will guide you through the available infusion sets, essential steps of replacing your insulin reservoir and infusion set properly, and adverse infusion site reactions.

### ***4.1 Different infusion sets***

While patch pumps usually work with 1 fixed cannula, tubed pumps offer a wider selection of infusion sets and tube lengths:

- Type of Cannula: You can choose between angled and straight plastic cannulas, or a straight steel cannula, each coming in various lengths. The standard choice is a straight plastic cannula that can remain in place for three days. However, for individuals frequently experiencing discomfort from a folded subcutaneous cannula or who have allergies, a steel cannula might be a more suitable alternative. Remember, steel cannulas generally need replacement every two days instead of three.

- Length and Angle of Cannula: Usually, the shortest cannula is selected, but if you notice insulin leakage at the infusion site, a longer or angled cannula might be a better solution. An angled cannula could be particularly beneficial for those with less subcutaneous fat or those who find straight cannulas uncomfortable.

- Tube Length: Tubed pumps allow the option to select tubes of different lengths, adding another layer of customization to your insulin delivery system.

- MiniMed has infusion sets that offer extended wear, lasting up to 7 days.

- Ypsopump’s mylife orbit infusion sets come with a unique feature - the tubing can rotate 360-degrees around the skin-colored adhesive patch.

An overview of all available infusion sets for tubed pumps can be found in the download section below this video. Remember, the goal is to choose an infusion set that ensures comfort, minimizes complications, and optimizes insulin delivery. Your healthcare provider can help guide your choice based on your lifestyle, physical characteristics, and personal preferences.

### ***4.2 Supplies***

Before starting, make sure you have the following items handy:

- Insulin: depending on the pump you use, you can use a pre-filled insulin PumpCart, or draw insulin from an insulin Penfill or Vial using a needle into the pump reservoir.

- Infusion set and tube: These might be bundled together or separate.

- An insertion system may be used to insert the infusion set into your skin

- And alcohol wipes or skin prep wipes for sanitization.

### ***4.3 Four-step procedure***

The process of changing an insulin reservoir and infusion set in a tube pump generally involves four stages:

- Refilling or swapping the insulin reservoir

- Filling the tubing

- Placing the infusion set

- And filling the cannula under the skin

If you're using a patch pump, there's no tubing to fill. You will have to fill the patch pump with insulin, follow the priming process, place the patch pump on your skin and insert the cannula.

After replacing your insulin and infusion set, it's crucial to check your blood sugar level about 2 hours later or keep an eye on your glucose sensor for the next few hours to make sure the change was successful. It’s best practice to avoid changing your infusion set before bedtime unless you're prepared to wake up two hours later to check your blood sugar level.

### ***4.4 Infusion site management***

Consider these guidelines when selecting and preparing an infusion site:

- Choose an Ideal Spot:

  - Opt for the abdomen, back of the upper arms, or lower back. Avoid the <span class="mark">upper legs due to unpredictable absorption rates, particularly during exercise.</span>

  - <span class="mark">On a limb, mount the patch pump vertically. The pump's window can point either direction. For the back, abdomen, or buttocks, position it horizontally or slightly angled.</span>

  - <span class="mark">Maintain a minimum distance of 5 cm or 2 inch from the navel and the last infusion site. Avoid placing under belts, near bones, or on scars, injuries, and skin conditions.</span>

  - <span class="mark">Rotate your injection sites regularly to prevent lipodystrophy, a condition that affects the fat under the skin. Creating a simple rotation schedule will help ensure consistency and reduce the risk of developing this problem.</span>

- <span class="mark">Preparation Steps:</span>

  - <span class="mark">Clean the skin with an alcohol wipe to ensure adherence. Allow it to air dry and ensure no soap or lotion residue or excess body hair.</span>

  - <span class="mark">Avoid applying sunscreen or insect repellent near the site as it could affect the pump's function.</span>

  - <span class="mark">For sensitive skin or allergies, opt for barrier wipes like Skin Prep or Conveen wipes, instead of alcohol wipes.</span>

  - <span class="mark">Consider additional adhesive like Skintac if the infusion set tends to come loose.</span>

  - <span class="mark">Slight discomfort upon insertion may occur. Ice or anesth</span>etic cream may help, but ongoing discomfort could indicate the cannula touching a nerve or muscle, necessitating removal and reinsertion.

- Routine Checkups:

  - Inspect your infusion site twice daily for signs of irritation or infection such as pain, swelling, redness, warmth or insulin leakage. In these cases, reposition the infusion set: "When in doubt, change it out".

### ***4.5 Do's & don’ts***

Here are some key points to ensure efficient insulin delivery and prevent issues:

- <span class="mark">The main risk associated with insulin pumps is ketoacidosis. Those new to using an insulin pump should be educated that if they experience prolonged hyperglycemia (for example more than 2 hours above 270 mg/dl or 15 mmol/l) without a clear reason, they should always consider a problem with the infusion set and not just sleep through it. The mantra is “if in doubt, change it out”.</span>

- <span class="mark">Managing Air Bubbles:</span>

  - <span class="mark">Let insulin reach room temperature</span> before filling the reservoir to minimize air bubbles.

  - Ensure you remove any large air bubbles during the filling process. Small bubbles won't harm, but an air bubble over 2.5 cm length equals a 0.5 unit insulin gap, so it's best to eliminate them.

- Timely Infusion Set Replacement:

  - Replace the plastic cannula every 3 days and steel cannula every 2 days. If you’re using an Extended Wear Infusion Set, replace it every 7 days.

  - Some pumps won't halt insulin delivery after 3 days, so set an alarm to avoid missing the replacement, or purposely put just the amount of insulin you expect to use for 3 days, plus the amount to fill the tubing, into the pump, to ensure you will change the set on time. Other pumps, like the Omnipod DASH and Omnipod 5, automatically stop after a set time.

  - Carefully remove infusion sets or pods to avoid skin irritation, and clean any leftover adhesive with soap and water or a specialized product.

- Choosing the Right Filling Volume:

  - Each type of tubing and cannula requires a specific filling volume as per its user guide.

  - Be aware that short steel cannulas might not need filling.

  - Do not overlook filling the cannula; otherwise, you may miss the initial 0.5 unit of insulin.

  - You can check the necessary filling volume of the cannula by looking at the manual of the infusion set or at the overview in the download section.

<!-- -->

- In the rare event of the infusion set coming loose from your body, you can use an overpatch to secure it more firmly. It is recommended to leave an opening in the overpatch to allow for disconnecting the infusion set when needed, such as during showering.

Remember, it's not uncommon to encounter issues during infusion set insertion, like a kinked cannula or premature insertion mechanism, but it's usually a minor setback. You can always restart with a new set. If you see blood post-insertion, reinsert the set. If you're out of practice, instructional videos on YouTube can be helpful. Good luck!

**<u>5. Pump alarms</u>**

Welcome to this lesson on insulin pump alarms.

We’ll discuss alarm types and some general do’s & don’ts.

Although these alarms vary across different pumps, they share certain common characteristics.

### ***5.1 Alarm types***

Insulin pumps generally categorize alarms into two priorities:

- High-priority alarms: These include an empty insulin reservoir, depleted battery, technical faults, etc., leading to a halt in insulin delivery.

- And Lower-priority alerts: These are typically related to a low insulin reservoir, low battery, blockages, etc. The pump continues insulin delivery but requests problem resolution.

Each alarm requires acknowledgment and action. If ignored, alarms will progressively get louder. You can often customize alarm volume and vibration settings and can temporarily silence lower-priority alarms. The pumps usually allow setting up one or more reminders.

### ***5.2 Do's & don’ts***

- Familiarize yourself with potential alarms to avoid panic when one occurs. Instruction manuals outline all alarms and the appropriate actions, so consider saving a digital copy on your phone.

- Contact the pump manufacturer's customer service department if needed, keeping in mind that not all offer 24/7 support. It’s worth creating a Contact in your mobile phone for your insulin pump company, and including their hours of operation as a note on the contact.

- Some alarms, like the "screaming pod" from Omnipod, can be tricky to deactivate without the remote control. In such cases, use a paperclip to disable the chip card through the pod's back opening.

- If you're temporarily not using your pump, switch it to storage mode to prevent constant alarms. Each pump has its unique procedure for this, outlined in its operating instructions.

- It's crucial to be aware that in the event of a pump malfunction, insulin delivery remains essential. To ensure preparedness, it is recommended to have your rapid- and long-acting insulin pens easily accessible as a backup. Create an emergency plan outlining the required insulin doses in case of pump failure, and make sure to update this plan regularly based on your current insulin needs. Being prepared for unexpected situations is vital, as pump problems can arise at any time.

- Additionally, it's wise to keep a record of all your basal rates and bolus ratios in a note on your phone, which is synced to the Cloud, or email yourself a copy whenever you make any changes. This way, if you need to set up a new pump, you will have a record of your settings readily available. Taking these precautions ensures you are well-prepared to handle any potential issues with your insulin pump effectively.

Now, you're equipped with some essential knowledge about pump alarms and how to respond. If an alarm triggers, stay calm and check the screen instructions or refer to the user manual or customer service. Good luck!
